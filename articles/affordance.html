Wayfinding

Wayfinding is the method by which you let users of your site know how to get to other parts of the site. For example, if you land on the homepage of Monocle and you know you want to subscribe to their physical print magazine the link is right there at the top of the page for you to get to that page.

Here are my 5 methods to ensure that your digital product has effective wayfinding:

Create comprehensive user journey flows.

This will make it clear which page you intend the user to go to next. On a ecommerce user journey, this will often be the next step of the funnel, eg. From basket to checkout. On an editorial website you may want a user to navigate from a section page to an article page. The user journey flows all provide the architecture you can build on to ensure users can find the next step.

Make use of typography on the page

Road signs in the UK use the Transport font, designed by Margaret Calvert in 1958. The use of a white font on a green background is insanely recognisable as a pointer to a location to drive to. You can use this same technique on the web. If your body copy is a serif, you could use a sans-serif for your wayfinding, or use a slightly different colour so that users know they are going to be taken to a new part of the website.

Limit the amount of signposts

Some websites are made up of thousands of different pages, and only a small percentage will be relevant to every user, so don’t present every option to every user. You should consider the objectives of each distinct user journey to ensure that the relevant options are displayed.

Use icons instead of words

Sometimes guiding users around your website can be done with simple symbols. Most users know what will happen when they tap on 3 vertical lines or a right facing arrow, which would require the words "Menu" or "Back". There are many examples of icon sets that you can use for free on your site but Google's Material design has a set of over XX icons that have sets for almost all website functionality and are designed to be consistent with each other.

Use gesture design

On iOS when you're scrolling through a table view, when you select an row the screen slides to the left to reveal the content. This creates a mental model in the user's head that to return to the list of options you need to navigate to the left, and when a left swipe gesture is used that is where the user does. By using interactions that creates a 2D map in the users head, you can let people know where they are on your site.
